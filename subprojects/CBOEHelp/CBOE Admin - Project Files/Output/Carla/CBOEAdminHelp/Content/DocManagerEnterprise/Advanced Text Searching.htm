Advanced Text Searching

Advanced text Searching narrows the search and retrieve precise results by the using of specific operators.

Escape Characters

To search for words or symbols that have special meaning to query expressions, such as & or |, you must escape them so that DocManager doesn't interpret them as search operators. There are two ways to escape characters in a query expression, using the curly brackets, {}and using a back slash, \.

Curly brackets

Any symbol or string within a set of curly brackets is considered a part of the escape sequence. When you use braces to escape a single character, the escaped character becomes a separate token in the query. The table below shows examples of how to use curly brackets to escape an ampersand (&) and a dash (-):

Instead of Use
AT&T {AT&T}
high-voltage {high-voltage}

back slash

The backlash symbol can be used to escape only a single character or a symbol. Only the character/symbol immediately following the back slash is escaped. The table below shows examples of how to use a back slash to escape an ampersand (&) and a dash (-):

Instead of Use
AT&T AT\&T
high-voltage high\-voltage

The various types of advanced text searching are:

  • ABOUT
  • AND (&)
  • EQUIValence (=)
  • fuzzy (?)
  • MINUS (-)
  • NEAR
  • NOT (~)
  • OR (|)
  • Soundex (!)
  • Stem ($)

ABOUT

The ABOUT operator, retrieves documents that contain information related to a word or phrase. Therefore, rather than searching for documents that contain specific words or phrases, you can search for documents that are about a certain subject, even if that subject is not mentioned explicitly in the document.

The ABOUT operator is useful when you have already built a knowledge base. For more information about building a suitable knowledge base for your installation, see the Oracle 9i Text guide.

The ABOUT operator is used, by entering the word ABOUT in all capital letters followed by the word or phrase on which to search in parentheses.

Searches using the ABOUT operator are case sensitive. For example, if you had added "carbon" and "carbon bi-products from synthesis at 25 degrees Celsius" to your knowledge base, the following table lists how your searches are interpreted:

Searching For... Returns...
ABOUT (carbon) Documents with words related to carbon in the documentâ€™s text. Matches include coal and diamond.
ABOUT (carbon bi-products from syntheses at 25 degrees Celsius) Documents with words related to "carbon bi-products from synthesis at 25 degrees Celsius".

The ABOUT operator becomes more effective when coupled with other operators, such as AND or NOT.

For example, if you had added "carbon" and "diamond" to your knowledge base, the table lists how your searches are interpreted:

Searching For... Returns...
ABOUT (carbon) AND diamond Documents with words related to carbon as well as the word diamond.
ABOUT (carbon) NOT ABOUT (diamond) Documents with words related to carbon but excluding the word diamond.

NOTE: For advanced Oracle users: The word or phrase specified in an ABOUT query does not have to exactly match the themes stored in the index. Oracle automatically normalizes the word or phrase before performing lookup in the Text index.

AND

The AND (&) operator, retrieves documents that contain more than one word or phrase. The AND operator is used to search for documents that contain all the search items that you specify. For a document to be returned, it must contain at least one instance of each item. The AND operator is used by typing the first term, the word AND or &, followed by another word or phrase.

Examples of using the AND operator are:

Searching For... Returns...
carbon AND diamond Documents that include at least one instance of "carbon" and "diamond".
carbon & diamond Documents that include at least one instance of "carbon" and "diamond".
carbon & diamond & graphite Documents that include at least one instance of "carbon", "diamond", and "graphite".
thymidine synthesis AND carbon dioxide Documents that include at least one instance of "thymidine synthesis" and "carbon dioxide".

EQUIValence

The EQUIValence (=) operator, lets you find documents that contain words that are interchangeable or similar. The EQUIValence operator is used to specify an acceptable substitution for a word in a query. The EQUIValence operator is used by writing EQUIV or =, followed by the phrase on which the search is to be performed.

The following table lists a few examples of using the EQUIValence operator:

Searching For... Returns...
graphite EQUIV diamond The words graphite or diamond occurring anywhere within a documentâ€™s text.

NOTE: The EQUIValent operator has higher precedence than all other operators except the expansion operators, such as fuzzy, soundex, and stem.

NOT

Use the NOT (~) operator to specify two terms so that the search retrieves documents that contain the first term, but not the second term. To use the NOT operator, type the term to be searched followed by either NOT or ~, and the term to be excluded.

Examples of using the NOT operator:

Searching For... Returns...
carbon NOT diamond Documents that include "carbon" but not "diamond".
carbon ~ diamond Documents that include "carbon" but not "diamond".
carbon NOT (diamond OR graphite) Documents that carbon but not diamond nor graphite.

NOTE: The NOT operator does not affect other logical operators.

OR

The OR (|) operator retrieves documents that contain one or more terms (where each term is a word or phrase) that you specify. Type the first term followed by OR or the symbol, |, followed by another term.
