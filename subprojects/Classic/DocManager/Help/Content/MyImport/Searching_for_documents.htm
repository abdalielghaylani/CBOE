Searching for documents

DocManager Enterprise supports structure searching and basic and advanced searching for text characters and operators. To perform a simple text search, see "Performing a search".
When performing a text search, you specify the search criteria by filling in values for the various fields in the query form. When performing a structure search, you draw a structure of the compound you are looking for. For more information about drawing structures, see the Chem & Bio Draw User guide.
After entering search criteria, click Search.

Performing a search

To search documents for specific information:
1. Click Search Documents in either the ChemBioOffice Manager main page or the DocManager Main Menu. The Document Search page appears:

 

2. Enter the search criteria by filling-in the various fields. The Full-Text text box allows to search exact word or phrases.For more information about drawing structures, please see “Chem & Bio Draw User Guide”.
3. Click Search. The search result appears:

 

4. Click the title of the appropriate document to view it in your web browser.
Note: You can also save a file displayed in the result by clicking on DownLoad corresponding to the file.

Basic text searching

When performing a basic text search operation, you can use a number of special characters to manipulate the search results. For example, you can narrow your search to ensure that the results are less ambiguous, precise, and correct.
To search, either enter the exact phrase you want to find or use a wildcard text string.

Exact phrase matching

Exact phrase matching lets you retrieve documents that contain the exact text specified in the search criteria. It is the most simple search, which returns exactly the requested information.
Examples of exact phrase matching are:

 

Exact Phrase

  

thymidine synthesis

The words thymidine synthesis in that order, with no words between in a document’s text.

cyclohexane

The word cyclohexane in a document’s text.

Wildcard searching

Where an exact search returns all instances of an entire word and phrase, a wildcard search returns all instances of a part of a word. An asterisk represents the wildcard that is returned and can be alphanumeric, a keyboard symbol, space, or nothing
Examples of Wildcard Searching are:

 

Searching For...

 

synth*

Any word with synth as the first five letters in a document’s text, such as synthesis, synthetic, and syntheses.

*ethane

Any word or phrase that ends with "ethane", such as ethane and methane.

Advanced text searching

Advanced text Searching narrows the search and retrieve precise results by the using of specific operators.

Escape Characters

To search for words or symbols that have special meaning to query expressions, such as & or |, you must escape them so that DocManager doesn't interpret them as search operators. There are two ways to escape characters in a query expression, using the curly brackets, {}and using a back slash, \.

Curly brackets

Any symbol or string within a set of curly brackets is considered a part of the escape sequence. When you use braces to escape a single character, the escaped character becomes a separate token in the query. The table below shows examples of how to use curly brackets to escape an ampersand (&) and a dash (-):

AT&T

{AT&T}

high-voltage

{high-voltage}

back slash

The backlash symbol can be used to escape only a single character or a symbol. Only the character/symbol immediately following the back slash is escaped. The table below shows examples of how to use a back slash to escape an ampersand (&) and a dash (-):

ABOUT

The ABOUT operator, retrieves documents that contain information related to a word or phrase. Therefore, rather than searching for documents that contain specific words or phrases, you can search for documents that are about a certain subject, even if that subject is not mentioned explicitly in the document.
The ABOUT operator is useful when you have already built a knowledge base. For more information about building a suitable knowledge base for your installation, see the Oracle 9i Text guide.
The ABOUT operator is used, by entering the word ABOUT in all capital letters followed by the word or phrase on which to search in parentheses.
Searches using the ABOUT operator are case-sensitive.
For example, if you had added "carbon" and "carbon bi-products from synthesis at 25 degrees Celsius" to your knowledge base, the following table lists how your searches are interpreted:

 

Searching For...

Returns...

ABOUT (carbon)

Documents with words related to carbon in the document’s text. Matches include coal and diamond.

ABOUT (carbon bi-products from syntheses at 25 degrees Celsius)

Documents with words related to "carbon bi-products from synthesis at 25 degrees Celsius".
The ABOUT operator becomes more effective when coupled with other operators, such as AND or NOT.
For example, if you had added "carbon" and "diamond" to your knowledgebase, the table lists how your searches are interpreted:

 

Searching For...

Returns...

ABOUT (carbon) AND diamond

Documents with words related to carbon as well as the word diamond.

ABOUT (carbon) NOT ABOUT (diamond)

Documents with words related to carbon but excluding the word diamond.
Note: For advanced Oracle users: The word or phrase specified in an ABOUT query does not have to exactly match the themes stored in the index. Oracle automatically normalizes the word or phrase before performing lookup in the Text index.

AND

The AND (&) operator, retrieves documents that contain more than one word or phrase. The AND operator is used to search for documents that contain all the search items that you specify. For a document to be returned, it must contain at least one instance of each item..
The AND operator is used by typing the first term, the word AND or &, followed by another word or phrase.
Examples of using the AND operator are:

 

Searching For...

Returns...

carbon AND diamond

Documents that include at least one instance of "carbon" and "diamond".

carbon & diamond

Documents that include at least one instance of "carbon" and "diamond".

carbon & diamond & graphite

Documents that include at least one instance of "carbon", "diamond", and "graphite".

thymidine synthesis AND carbon dioxide

Documents that include at least one instance of "thymidine synthesis" and "carbon dioxide".

EQUIValence

The EQUIValence (=) operator, lets you find documents that contain words that are interchangeable or similar. The EQUIValence operator is used to specify an acceptable substitution for a word in a query.
The EQUIValence operator is used by writing EQUIV or =, followed by the phrase on which the search is to be performed.
The following table lists a few examples of using the EQUIValence operator:

 

Searching For...

Returns...

graphite EQUIV diamond

The words graphite or diamond occurring anywhere within a document’s text.

graphite = diamond

The words graphite or diamond occurring anywhere within a document’s text.

carbon dioxide = monoxide

The words carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, or both terms occurring anywhere within a document’s text.
Note: The EQUIValent operator has higher precedence than all other operators except the expansion operators, such as fuzzy, soundex, and stem.

Searching For...

Returns...

?boron

Documents that include any word that is similar to 'boron'. Matches may include baron.

?read

Documents that include any word that is similar to 'read'. Matches include read, lead, and real.

?chemist

Documents that include any word that is similar to 'chemist'. Matches include chemists and chemistry.

Searching For...

 Returns...

carbon - diamond

Documents containing only the word carbon.

carbon MINUS diamond

Documents containing only the word carbon.

diamond -carbon

Documents containing only the word diamond.

Searching For...

Returns...

carbon NEAR diamond

The words carbon and diamond in documents where these words appear less than 100 words apart.

Searching For...

Returns...

NEAR ((carbon, diamond), 20, FALSE)

The words carbon and diamond in documents where these words appear less than 20 words apart, in no specific order.

NEAR ((carbon, diamond), 20, TRUE)

The words carbon and diamond in documents where these words appear less than 20 words apart, in this specific order.

NEAR ((carbon, diamond), 10) AND benzene

The words carbon, diamond, and benzene in documents where carbon and diamond appear less than 10 words apart and in no specific order.

NEAR ((carbon, diamond = graphite), 10)

The words carbon, diamond, and graphite in documents where carbon and diamond or carbon and graphite appear less than 10 words apart and in no specific order.

NOT

Use the NOT (~) operator to specify two terms so that the search retrieves documents that contain the first term, but not the second term. To use the NOT operator, type the term to be searched followed by either NOT or ~, and the term to be excluded.
Examples of using the NOT operator:

 

Searching For...

Returns...

carbon NOT diamond

Documents that include "carbon" but not "diamond".

carbon ~ diamond

Documents that include "carbon" but not "diamond".

carbon NOT (diamond OR graphite)

Documents that carbon but not diamond nor graphite.
Note: The NOT operator does not affect other logical operators.

OR

The OR (|) operator retrieves documents that contain one or more terms ( where each term is a word or phrase) that you specify. Type the first term followed by OR or the symbol, |, followed by another term.
Examples of using the OR operator:

 

Searching For...

Returns...

carbon OR diamond

The words carbon, diamond, or both appearing anywhere in the document’s text.

carbon | diamond

The words carbon, diamond, or both appearing anywhere in the document’s text.

carbon OR diamond OR graphite

The words carbon, diamond, graphite, or any combination of the terms appearing anywhere in the document’s text

thymidine synthesis OR carbon dioxide

The words thymidine synthesis, carbon dioxide, or both terms appearing anywhere in the document’s text.

Searching For...

Returns...

$commit

Any word with the root 'commit' appearing in the document’s text. Matches include commits, committing, committee, and committed.

$chemist

Any word with the root 'chemist' appearing in the document’s text. Matches include chemist, chemistry, and chemists.
